Who I Am

About Me

I'm a firmware engineer who got hooked on embedded systems because of what happens at the hardware boundary — where software stops being abstract and starts controlling something real.

What I love most is communication protocols. CAN, UART, SPI, I2C — there's something deeply satisfying about understanding exactly how two chips talk to each other, getting the timing right, and watching data move reliably across a bus. At Bosch I spent months inside CANoe tracing ECU communication on DASy, and at Elecsis I wrote production drivers for half a dozen protocols from scratch. That's the work I want to keep doing.

I'm currently pursuing my M.S. in Electrical Engineering at the University of Cincinnati and looking for firmware roles where I can go deep on low-level systems — ideally in automotive, medical, or semiconductor hardware.

Adaptive Cruise Control System

The Q-Car had no way of knowing how close it was getting to an obstacle — it would just keep moving. I needed it to detect, judge distance, and adjust speed on its own. I used an Intel RealSense D435 depth camera for perception and built a PI controller in MATLAB/Simulink that took live distance readings and translated them into throttle commands. The tricky part was tuning the controller — too aggressive and the car would oscillate, too slow and it wouldn't brake in time. Getting that balance right taught me more about real-time control systems than any textbook had. When it finally decelerated smoothly and held a safe following distance on its own, that was the moment I knew it worked.

MATLAB Simulink PI Controller RealSense D435

Lane Detection & Vehicle Guidance

The challenge with lane detection isn't just finding lines — it's finding the right lines under varying lighting, curves, and road noise. I built a pipeline using Canny edge detection to isolate boundaries and Hough Transform to extract lane geometry. From there, I computed vanishing points and lane ratios to determine the vehicle's heading in real time. What I learned: perception problems are never as clean as they look in demos. A lot of the work was handling edge cases where shadows or faded markings would confuse the pipeline — and building logic robust enough to recover gracefully.

Python OpenCV Canny Edge Hough Transform
03
🗺️

Path Planning — RRT Algorithms

Given a map with obstacles, how does a robot find a safe path without exhaustively searching every possibility? That question led me to implement RRT, RRT*, and Bi-RRT* in MATLAB. Basic RRT finds a path but doesn't care how inefficient it is. RRT* keeps refining toward the optimal path as it explores. Bi-RRT* grows trees from both start and goal simultaneously — dramatically faster in tight spaces. Benchmarking all three side by side made the trade-offs concrete: you're always choosing between exploration speed and path quality. That insight directly shapes how I think about algorithm selection in constrained embedded environments.

MATLAB RRT* Bi-RRT* Path Planning
04
💊

EEPROM Digital Dose Counter

This was a medical device — a dose counter for Asthma and COPD patients. The constraint that mattered most: it runs on a battery, and it cannot lose data if power cuts out mid-dose. I solved the persistence problem by writing dose counts to EEPROM after every actuation, so a power cycle could never corrupt the record. For battery life, I pushed the MCU into sleep mode between uses, waking only on actuation events. The result was 40% better battery life and zero data loss across power cycles. Building something that a patient depends on daily changed how seriously I take reliability in firmware.

Embedded C EEPROM ARM Medical IoT

Arduino-Based Radar System

I wanted to build something that visualized space — not just detect an object but show where it is. I mounted an ultrasonic sensor on a DC motor and swept it in an arc, computing angle and distance at each step. The data fed into a custom display that rendered a live radar sweep. Adding wireless control via an HC-05 Bluetooth module was the part that pushed me — suddenly the system had to handle asynchronous serial input while still running the sweep loop without blocking. That concurrency challenge was my first real taste of writing interrupt-driven embedded code, and it stuck with me.

Arduino Embedded C HC-05 Ultrasonic

👥

Social Distancing Monitor — Deep Learning

During COVID, enforcing distancing in public spaces was a real problem with no scalable solution. My team decided to build one. We used YOLOv3 to detect people in a video feed and the Centroid Algorithm to track and measure inter-person distances frame by frame. Violations triggered an alert via a GSM module. The hardest part was making YOLO run fast enough on our hardware to be useful in real time — we had to make deliberate trade-offs between detection accuracy and inference speed. Leading a team through that problem taught me as much about communication and decision-making under constraints as it did about computer vision.

Python YOLO V3 TensorFlow OpenCV Keras
